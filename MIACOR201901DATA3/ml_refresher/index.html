Introduction To Machine Learning

Before we get started with machine learning, we need to understand some basic concepts and principles first.

Machine learning isn't mystical. In my opinion, it's real-time statistics that is made possible on today's machinery. You will realize that many of the algorithms we have today are based on the foundations of statistical and mathematical discoveries in the past.

In school, we had to learn about sampling. That's because it's not always possible to gather every piece of data necessary to predict. For example:

  • Let's pretend you work in a company which makes condoms.
  • You're a QA analyst who's required to assess the reliability of a product.
  • To test your analysis, you can't possibly be stress-testing all the condoms in your inventory to test for reliability. You won't have anything left to sell!

Sampling allows us to extrapolate a scenario, where probably 1 out of 10,000 products, there is one which is faulty. This is also foundational for predictive analytics.

However, recent development of technology is coming to the point where we are relying less on sampling, and we have the machinery to ingest tons of data for analysis. Thus, this is the beginnings of data science and machine learning.

Basic Definitions

  • Intelligent Algorithms
    • Algorithms which enhances itself based on the data given to make informed decisions.
  • Within Intelligent Algorithms, there are:
    • Machine Learning
    • Predictive Analytics
    • Artificial Intelligence

Artificial Intelligence

Agents that can perceive environments and make decisions to maximize its chance for achieving its goals.

Examples:

  • Google DeepMind for StarCraft 2

Predictive Analytics

Based on patterns and recurring behavior, we extrapolate the chance of the situation happening again, and we optimize our business objectives in relation to the data given.

Examples:

  • Retargeting ads: If you're going to buy coffee from Amazon, it's likely that you're going to buy coffee again from Amazon. So we show you new coffee products on Amazon.

Machine Learning

There are different categories of machine learning as follows:

  • Unsupervised learning: You observe the data to interpret itself, and let that dictate your parameters to obtain accuracy.
    • Clustering: Finding groups within a population
  • Supervised learning: You already know the outcome you want, and so you determine the parameters for your data to boost accuracy.
    • Classification: Grouping data into buckets with similar properties
    • Regression: Fitting data to determine if an event would happen again.
  • Reinforcement learning: It's more for AI systems, but the data which you output can feed itself into the model, and it enhances your existing accuracy to accomplish a goal.

In machine learning, we always do the model-fit-predict model. You will see that often in your student activities.

  • Model: Finding the right algorithm and set of features from the data to determine the accuracy of your outcome.
  • Fit: Using what you have modeled, you're using data (usually historical data) to train the model in order to achieve an acceptable level of accuracy.
  • Predict: After training the model, we input raw data from the environment into it and it will determine the outcome based on the model.

This linear graph is to simulate a probable scenario. For example:

  • We understand that the larger the house, the more expensive it is. (Let's ignore the other factors like traffic and environment.)

Mathematical Formula

It's going back to high school, but:

y = mx + b
  • y is the output response
  • x is the input feature (we have one feature, remember? Square fit is the feature for our example.)
  • b is the y-axis intercept.
  • m is the gradient, or weight coefficient.

Quantifying Regression

This just means how do we prove if our model works? How do we quantify accuracy?

As we use linear regression as our foundational study material, realize that what you learned here is going to be applied to other models as well.

To quantify accuracy, we use common scoring metrics such as:

  • R-Squared (R2)
  • Mean Squared Error (MSE)

R2 Concept

We use the margin of errors to depict the accuracy of a model. Consider this graph:

We're trying to measure the accuracy of our model by using the margin of errors. Thus:

  • Having the same input (x1), the predicted data would feature on the line, and we have the actual results as (x1,y1).
  • The margin of error, r, would be y1 - y since there is a difference between the 2 output values.
  • Squaring them eliminates negatives (high school math), and we get an area on the graph.
  • We can't base our accuracy on a single margin of error. Thus, since all points have their margin of errors, we add them all up to get the Squared-Error of the line (SEline).
  • But the SEline doesn't allow us to compare with anything. So, we calculate the total variation in y to quantify how the margin of error against the total variance of y.
  • By division, SEline/SEy-mean, we get the amount of error in relation to the total variance of y. But this describes the error percentage. How about the accuracy?
  • Accuracy is described by 100%, or 1 if we're dealing with decimals. Thus, 1 - SEline/SEy-mean will give us an accuracy score, and it's often described as the coefficient of determination.
